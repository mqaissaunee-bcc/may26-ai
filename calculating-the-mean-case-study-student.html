Why Can't Maya Calculate the Mean?

A 30-minute diagnostic investigation. You will examine evidence from a study session, identify what is going wrong, rule out one easy-to-miss alternative, and recommend a corrective step.

1 Overview

Read the framing before opening the artifacts.

Discipline: Math (Intro Statistics) Setting: Campus tutoring center Time frame: One 20-minute session Acuity: Time-sensitive (quiz in 1 hour)

The situation

It is 1:15 PM on a Tuesday. Maya, a first-semester student, walks into the campus tutoring center looking flustered. She has a short statistics quiz at 2:30 PM and the practice problems she just attempted are all wrong. The instructor has already given her the answer key, but she cannot match her work to it. Her notebook is open, her calculator is on, and she is convinced she "just isn't a math person."

You have access to her practice worksheet, her scratch work, two short notes from her instructor and tutor, and a brief transcript of what she said when she sat down. You have roughly 20 minutes before she has to leave for class.

Your role. You are a peer math tutor on a diagnostic call. Your job is not to teach the whole topic from scratch — it is to figure out exactly what is breaking down in Maya's process, rule out one alternative explanation that looks similar, and recommend a single corrective step she can apply before her quiz.

5 Capstone Assignment

Pull everything together into a short decision memo.

Decision memo: "What is going wrong, and what should Maya do in the next 30 minutes?"

Audience: Maya herself, plus the math instructor (Prof. Chen) cc'd. Length: 200–300 words. Format: Three short paragraphs.

In paragraph 1, name the specific error you identified and cite the artifact IDs (e.g., A2, A3) that show it. In paragraph 2, name the alternative explanation you considered and ruled out, and say briefly why the evidence does not support it. In paragraph 3, give Maya one concrete corrective step she can apply to problems 4–6 before her 2:30 quiz.

Grading rubric (10 points)

  • Accuracy (3 pts): The error you name is actually present in the artifacts.
  • Evidence (3 pts): You cite specific artifact IDs and quote or paraphrase the relevant detail.
  • Differential reasoning (2 pts): You name a plausible alternative and explain why it is ruled out.
  • Actionable recommendation (1 pt): Your corrective step is something Maya can do in 5 minutes, not a week-long study plan.
  • Tone and clarity (1 pt): Respectful, plain language; no jargon dropped on Maya without a quick definition.

6 Glossary

Key terms used in the case, with one-sentence definitions.

Mean (arithmetic mean)
The sum of all values in a data set divided by the number of values; often called the "average."
Median
The middle value when a data set is arranged in order from smallest to largest; for an even-sized set, the average of the two middle values.
Mode
The value that appears most often in a data set; a set can have no mode, one mode, or several.
Data set
The collection of numerical values you are working with for a single problem.
Sum
The result of adding all values together.
Count (n)
How many individual values are in the data set; the divisor when calculating a mean.
Order of operations
The agreed-upon sequence (parentheses, exponents, multiplication and division, addition and subtraction) for evaluating a math expression.
Outlier
A value that sits far from the rest of the data and can pull the mean noticeably higher or lower.
Diagnostic question
A targeted question a tutor or teacher asks to figure out exactly where a student's reasoning is breaking down.
Procedural error
A mistake in the steps of a calculation (such as dividing by the wrong number), as opposed to a misunderstanding of the underlying concept.
